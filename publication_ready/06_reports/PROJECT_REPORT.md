# Project Report

## Title

In Silico Identification and Structural Characterisation of a Putative
Polyurethanase from Pestalotiopsis microspora

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Background

Polyurethane pollution is a growing environmental problem.

Russell et al. (2011) reported that Pestalotiopsis microspora can
degrade polyester polyurethane and identified a secreted approximately
21 kDa serine hydrolase-like enzyme.

However, the molecular identity of this enzyme was unknown.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Objective

To identify a possible polyurethane degrading enzyme candidate from the
Pestalotiopsis microspora genome and investigate its structural and
substrate-binding properties.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Dataset

NCBI genome:

Pestalotiopsis microspora KFRD-2

Accession:

GCA_036323685.1

BioProject:

PRJNA1059488

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Methodology

The workflow included:

1.  Genome acquisition
2.  Protein extraction
3.  Size-based filtering
4.  Secretome prediction
5.  Serine hydrolase domain screening
6.  Structure prediction
7.  Active site analysis
8.  Molecular docking

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Results

A final candidate:

g13490.t1

was analysed.

Docking showed strongest predicted interaction with:

MDI fragment

Binding affinity:

-7.19 kcal/mol

Compared with DMSO control:

-2.68 kcal/mol

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Interpretation

The candidate protein showed favourable interaction with
polyurethane-related ligands.

This supports its potential role as a polyurethane degrading enzyme
candidate.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Conclusion

This computational study provides a possible molecular identity for the
enzyme activity described by Russell et al. and creates a foundation for
experimental validation.
